Chapter 01 — The Problem

Opacity in the Age of Algorithmic Decisions

Artificial intelligence is no longer a futuristic concept. It is actively diagnosing patients, assessing criminal defendants, screening job applicants, and guiding autonomous vehicles. The concern is not that AI is being used. The concern is that most people, including the professionals relying on it, cannot see inside how it reaches its conclusions.

This is often called the "black box" problem. AI systems accept inputs and produce outputs, but their internal logic is hidden, often even from their own creators. In low-stakes situations this may not matter much. In high-stakes environments, it becomes a serious ethical problem.

"As AI and ML become more widespread and intertwined with human-centric applications, and algorithmic decisions become more consequential to individuals and society, attention has shifted back from accuracy to explainability."

— Angelov et al., 2021 (WIREs Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery)

Pushing AI into decision-critical roles without adequate transparency creates gaps in accountability, fairness, and public safety. When an AI makes an incorrect decision, there is no clear reasoning trail to follow. It becomes nearly impossible to identify what went wrong, who bears responsibility, or how to stop it from happening again.

Chapter 02 — Ethical Issues

Three Core Ethical Failures

Click each card to learn about the specific ethical challenges that come with opaque AI systems.

🏛️
Trust & Accountability
+

When AI makes decisions in professional settings like medicine, law, or finance without any transparency, the public loses trust in those institutions. More dangerously, there is no clear answer to the question of who is responsible when the AI is wrong.

The Carnegie Council defines AI accountability as ensuring "responsibility for bad outcomes can be assigned to liable parties." Without transparent AI, that assignment becomes nearly impossible. Victims are left without recourse while organizations face little pressure to do better.

Pazzanese (2024, Harvard Gazette) notes that this lack of accountability tends to hurt the victim most, while the institution that deployed the AI often faces little to no consequence.

Harvard Gazette, 2024 Carnegie Council, 2026
🚨
Public Safety
+

Opaque AI in safety-critical fields can have life-or-death consequences. Three sectors are especially exposed:

Medicine: An AI that misdiagnoses a condition or fails to flag a disease without any explanation takes away the doctor's ability to question or override it (Fehr et al., 2024).

Autonomous Vehicles: When a self-driving car fails to detect a pedestrian or makes a bad ethical call in a collision scenario, a missing decision log makes it impossible to understand or correct what went wrong (UNESCO, 2026).

Criminal Justice: AI risk assessment tools used in sentencing and bail decisions have been found to carry hidden biases. People's freedom can be extended or denied based on faulty algorithms that no one can audit (MacKenzie, 2025).

Fehr et al., 2024 UNESCO, 2026 MacKenzie, 2025
⚖️
Discrimination & Bias
+

AI systems learn from historical data, and history is not neutral. Societal biases around gender, race, and disability that exist in training data get carried over and often amplified by the resulting algorithms.

A landmark study in dermatology (Adamson & Smith, 2018) found that AI algorithms struggled significantly to identify melanoma in patients with darker skin tones. This was a direct result of training data that underrepresented people of color.

A systematic review (Murikah et al., 2024) identified five sources of algorithmic bias:

① Data Deficiencies  ② Demographic Homogeneity  ③ Spurious Correlations  ④ Improper Comparators  ⑤ Cognitive Biases

Without transparency, these biases stay invisible and go unchallenged.

Adamson & Smith, 2018 Murikah et al., 2024

Chapter 03 — Impact

Who Bears the Cost?

The consequences of non-transparent AI do not fall evenly across society. Understanding who gets hurt, and how, is essential to building any meaningful solution.

Directly Affected

Marginalized Communities

People of color, women, and individuals with disabilities are most at risk. Pre-existing societal biases that are embedded in training data get carried into algorithmic decision-making, often in amplified form.

The broader public is also directly affected. AI now shapes outcomes in healthcare, transportation, employment, and the justice system, all institutions that most people interact with throughout their lives.

Indirectly Affected

Institutions & Companies

Organizations that deploy opaque AI face serious reputational damage when that AI makes high-profile errors. For-profit entities like law firms, automotive companies, and insurers also risk financial losses.

The Holistic AI Team (2024) notes that AI bias can lead to discrimination lawsuits, loss of consumer trust, and direct financial setbacks for implementing organizations.

Chapter 04 — The Solution Emerging

Explainable AI (XAI)

In response to the "black box" problem, the field has been moving toward Explainable AI (XAI), an approach that requires AI systems to provide clear, human-understandable reasoning for their decisions. XAI is built on three core requirements:

🔍
Transparency

The AI must provide an explanation of how it arrived at its decision. This is the direct opposite of "black box" behavior.

🧩
Interpretability

The explanation must be understandable to humans, not just engineers. The professionals and patients relying on the system need to follow the reasoning too.

✅
Explainability

The explanation must accurately reflect the AI's actual decision process and be comprehensible to its intended audience.

Figure: A comparison of XAI and Black Box AI. The top panel shows how XAI produces explainable outputs applied in healthcare, finance, and legal systems. The bottom panel shows how black-box AI produces unclear outputs in applications like face recognition, speech recognition, and recommendation systems.

NIST's Four Principles of XAI

NIST has outlined four principles that any genuinely explainable AI system should meet:

  1. Explanation
    The AI provides evidence and reasoning behind every decision it makes.
  2. Meaningful
    The reasoning should be in language that fits the user. Non-technical for everyday users, clinical for doctors, legal for judges.
  3. Accurate
    The reasoning shown must genuinely reflect how the AI actually made the decision, not a cleaned-up explanation added afterward.
  4. Knowledge Limits
    The AI should acknowledge when its answers may not be reliable or when a question falls outside what it can confidently handle.

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) has pushed developers toward more transparent models, particularly in applications that involve personal data.

— Angelov et al., 2021

Current Challenges in XAI Adoption

XAI is starting to appear in medicine, criminal justice, and autonomous driving. Wide adoption, though, is still held back by several real obstacles:

📏 No standardized metrics 🗃️ Inconsistent data quality ⚙️ Integration into live systems ⚖️ Unresolved liability questions 📜 Gaps in legislation
Chapter 05 — Our Proposal

A Human-in-the-Loop Framework

Our team proposes combining XAI with structured human oversight. Instead of trusting AI outputs without question, or removing AI from the process entirely, we suggest adding a qualified professional review layer that uses XAI's transparency to audit decisions before they take effect.

The Proposed Solution

A qualified professional in the relevant field, such as a physician, legal expert, or engineer, reviews each AI decision that carries real consequences. Using the reasoning provided by XAI, they check that:

① The decision is not based on biased or low-quality data
② The reasoning aligns with current professional standards and regulations
③ The decision would withstand ethical and legal scrutiny

Ethical Frameworks That Guide This Proposal

Human Rights & Equality

The solution aims to reduce AI-driven discrimination, especially in high-impact decisions like hiring and medical diagnosis where marginalized communities tend to be affected most.

Utilitarianism

The solution is designed to benefit as many people as possible. It also honestly acknowledges that human reviewers take on extra responsibility, which is a real cost worth naming.

Justice

The goal is to ensure decisions are as fair as possible and not distorted by gaps or biases in the data the model was originally trained on.

Information Literacy

Having professionals review decisions also ensures that outputs are grounded in current, accurate knowledge from the field, not just whatever was in the training data at the time.

Projected Impacts

✓ Reduced discriminatory outcomes ✓ Clearer lines of accountability ✓ Increased public trust ✓ Regulatory compliance ~ Added review time & cost ✗ Risk of moral scapegoating

The Scapegoat Risk: Research by Robert Ranisch (2024) warns that inserting humans into AI oversight loops can backfire. When something goes wrong, those reviewers are often blamed for malfunctions they had no real control over, creating a new ethical problem even while trying to solve the original one.

— Ranisch, 2024 (The American Journal of Bioethics)

Policy Recommendations

Individual organizations should adopt frameworks like NIST's AI Risk Management Framework or ISO/IEC 42001, and use techniques such as LIME and SHAP to make their algorithms more transparent. Governments need to go further than simply citing international standards. They should write explicit, enforceable explainability requirements into domestic law, with clear and agreed-upon definitions. (Nannini et al., 2023)
